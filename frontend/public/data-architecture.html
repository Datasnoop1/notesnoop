DataSnoop · How the data is wired

From your search box to the 10-digit number that holds it all together

DataSnoop combines four very different data sources — a government registry, a central bank, a legal gazette, and an AI scribe — into a single searchable view of every Belgian company. This page explains how, layer by layer.

TL;DR — the whole system in 4 lines

  • Every Belgian company has one 10-digit ID number (the CBE). That number is the spine of the whole database.
  • Four data feeds — KBO, NBB, Staatsblad, AI — keep posting fresh sticky-notes against that ID.
  • The website is a Next.js front end + a FastAPI back end + one PostgreSQL database. No magic, no Elasticsearch.
  • Search is plain Postgres with a few clever indexes. Profiles are pre-pivoted so they read in one query.

1. The big picture

Forget databases for a minute. Here's the mental model.

The trading-card metaphor

Imagine a giant wall of trading cards, one per Belgian company. About 1.94 million cards. Each card has a unique 10-digit number stamped at the top — that number is the CBE (Crossroads Bank for Enterprises).

Now imagine four pen-pals who keep mailing you sticky-notes. Each note has the CBE number written on it, plus one piece of information:

  • The government sends notes with names, addresses, and legal status.
  • The National Bank sends notes with last year's revenue and profit.
  • The Official Gazette sends notes when a director resigns or a bankruptcy is filed.
  • An AI helper sends a plain-English description of what the company actually does.

Your job is to file each sticky-note onto the right card. That's it. That's the whole product. When a user searches, you grab the matching card and read off all the sticky-notes attached to it.

The 10-digit CBE is the magic. Without it, the notes would just be scattered paper.

The headline universe is ~685K filer-form companies — the NVs, BVs, BVBAs, CVBAs and other juridical forms legally required to deposit annual accounts. Of those, 185K have filed at least once since the JSON-XBRL window opened in April 2022; the rest haven't filed yet (or filed pre-2022 in PDF-only form). The 1.94M figure includes sole traders, foreign branches, and public bodies that are in KBO but not in scope for filing.

2. The four data sources

Where every fact actually comes from.

Source 1 — KBO

The Belgian government registry

What it gives us: the existence of every company. Names, addresses, legal form (NV, BV, VZW…), start date, NACE activity codes, establishment units.

How we get it: a giant ZIP file the government publishes monthly + smaller daily update ZIPs. We stream-parse them straight into Postgres so we never load the whole thing into memory.

Tables it fills: enterprise, denomination, address, activity, contact, establishment, branch.
Refreshed: daily at 03:00 UTC.
Licence note: personal data may NOT be used for direct marketing — that constraint shapes a lot of design decisions.
Source 2 — NBB

The National Bank of Belgium

What it gives us: the financials. Annual accounts — revenue, EBITDA, balance sheet, debt, headcount — plus directors and shareholders extracted from the structured XBRL filing.

How we get it: per-company REST API call, one company at a time, with a 1.5-second polite delay. Subscription-key authenticated (and the keys rotate without warning — there's a watchdog cron for that).

Tables it fills: financial_data (raw rubric codes), financial_latest, financial_by_year (pre-pivoted for speed), administrator, shareholder, participating_interest.
Refreshed: nightly batch + a continuous backload daemon that drips through the gap during the day.
Coverage gap: only XBRL filings since April 2022, and only entities legally required to file. Tiny partnerships are invisible.
Source 3 — Staatsblad

The Belgian Official Gazette

What it gives us: legal news. Director appointments and resignations, capital increases, mergers, bankruptcies, share transfers. Plus — newly — the home address of the people involved.

How we get it: we scrape the gazette daily, OCR the PDFs, then run them through Claude Haiku 4.5 in batches to extract structured events (eight categories: admin_event, capital_event, share_transfer…).

Tables it fills: staatsblad_publication, staatsblad_publication_text, staatsblad_event, staatsblad_event_embedding.
Refreshed: every 2 days; embeddings nightly at 05:45 UTC.
Why it matters: NBB filings are once a year. The gazette is how we catch real-time corporate changes between filings.
Source 4 — AI enrichment

The plain-English layer

What it gives us: a human-readable answer to "what does this company actually do?" — products, customer segments, business model. Without this, you'd just see NACE codes like "47.21" and shrug.

How we get it: two pipelines. A bulk one (cheap GPT-4o-mini) runs over the whole 1.94M corpus, escalates to Haiku 4.5 only when needed. A narrative one runs on demand when a user opens a profile page.

Tables it fills: company_enrichment.bulk_summary, company_embedding (for semantic search), ai_insights (longer narrative), translation_cache.
Refreshed: bulk worker drains a Postgres queue continuously with a $10/day budget guard.
Tradeoff: output quality depends on website scraping — no website, less to summarise.

3. The 10-digit glue

If you remember one thing from this page, remember this.

Every fact about a Belgian company in DataSnoop is keyed by its CBE number — 10 digits, no dots, no spaces. The first digit is usually a 0, then 9 more digits.

Why this matters

Because the CBE is a universal join key, the database can answer "show me everything about company X" with a small fan-out of fast lookups instead of a giant join across the universe.

Where the join keys get tricky

The CBE is reliable for companies. It is not reliable for people. Belgium does not expose a national ID for natural persons in any open dataset, so "Jan Janssens director at Company A" and "Jan Janssens director at Company B" are linked only by name + (now) home city / postcode harvested from Staatsblad publications.

That's why the people-search endpoint groups results by (lower(name), domicile_city, domicile_postcode). Two people with the same name in different cities show as two rows. Same name + same city → still one row, with a tradeoff toward false merges.

The CBE format gotcha

KBO renders the number as 0400.378.485. NBB wants 0400378485. Some users paste BE0400378485. DataSnoop strips dots, spaces and the BE prefix on every input, then stores a single canonical form: 10 raw digits.

Search input also handles 9-digit pastes (KBO sometimes drops the leading zero) by also probing digits[1:] as a prefix.

Three pre-computed shortcuts you should know about

TableWhat it pre-computesWhy
company_info One row per CBE with name, city, zipcode, nace_code So search and the screener don't have to join 5 tables on every keystroke

5. Real example: searching "Colruyt"

Step-by-step, what actually happens between keystroke and screen.

  1. You type "Colruyt" in the header search bar
    150 ms after the last keystroke, the browser fires GET /api/search/suggest?q=Colruyt. If you keep typing, the previous request gets cancelled — the browser uses an AbortController so a slow response from keystroke 3 never overwrites keystroke 7's results.
  2. nginx routes /api/* to the FastAPI backend
    Two middlewares fire first: a bot filter (rejects obvious crawlers) and a tier-limit middleware (does this user / this anonymous IP still have search calls left today?). If yes, the request continues.
  3. The backend normalises your text
    "Colruyt" → strip accents, lowercase, strip trailing legal suffixes (NV, SA, BV, SPRL…) → colruyt. Same function in SQL and Python so they always agree.
  4. One SQL round-trip with four parallel arms
    The query checks: (1) does company_info.name_normalized equal "colruyt" exactly? (2) start with it? (3) is it a CBE number? (4) does it match a denomination / trade name? Each arm has a base score; the highest wins.
  5. Postgres returns ~5 hits in 8–20 ms
    The trick: a GIN trigram index on name_normalized means substring matches don't scan the table. The dropdown renders.
  6. You click "Colruyt Group"
    Browser navigates to /company/0400378485. That URL is a Next.js Server Component; it calls the backend server-side (using API_URL_INTERNAL inside the Docker network) to pre-render the profile.
  7. The profile fan-out
    The backend pulls in parallel: company_info for the header, financial_latest + financial_by_year for the P&L chart, administrator for the people tab, staatsblad_event for recent legal events, company_enrichment.bulk_summary for the AI description. All keyed by the same CBE.
  8. The narrative AI tab loads on demand
    The longer "Summary" tab uses a separate pipeline that calls OpenRouter on the fly — but only once per company; the answer is cached in ai_insights for next time.
  9. Every step is logged
    activity_log gets one row per /api/* call — user email if logged in, hashed IP if anonymous. That's how tier limits count, and how the admin panel knows what's getting hit.
End-to-end latency on a warm cache, from keystroke to a rendered profile: roughly 200–700 ms. The slowest step is usually the AI summary, which is why it's split off into a tab that loads after the main page is already painted.

How the stack is deployed

Three Docker containers per environment: backend, frontend, nginx. Production and staging both live on the same Hetzner VPS, in separate docker-compose projects (leadpeek vs leadpeek-staging). Both connect to the same Postgres database — there is no staging DB. (See the architect-review section below for why this matters.)

Auth and tier model

  1. Supabase issues a JWT on Google or email login.
  2. Browser sends Authorization: Bearer <jwt> on every API call.
  3. FastAPI verifies it with a JWKS cache (1-hour TTL).
  4. A single TierLimitMiddleware classifies the endpoint and checks today's count in activity_log against the user's daily cap (anon, pro, admin).
  5. Free users can read everything. Limits only kick in on costly operations (AI calls, bulk exports). This is an explicit product decision, not a bug.

How searches actually rank

Pure Postgres — no Elasticsearch, no Meilisearch. Three extensions do the heavy lifting:

  • unaccent — strips accents so "Brüssels" matches "brussels".
  • pg_trgm — trigram similarity for fuzzy / substring matching.
  • fuzzystrmatch — Double-Metaphone phonetic keys, so "Braet" and "Brait" collide.

The company-search endpoint scores six candidate "arms" (exact name, prefix, multi-token AND, denomination match, fuzzy trigram, address match) and unions them. Highest score wins. Active companies get a x1.0 multiplier; struck-off companies get x0.3.

The semantic search layer

Each company has a 256-dimensional vector in company_embedding, computed from its bulk summary. When a user asks a fuzzy question ("a Belgian roof-tile manufacturer"), the query is also embedded and the answer is a cosine-distance nearest-neighbour search via pgvector's HNSW index (m=16, ef_construction=64). Query embeddings are cached for 30 days in query_embedding_cache — same question, no second OpenRouter bill.

7. Senior architect review

Where the system is solid, where it's fragile, and what would break first at 10× scale.

What's well-designed

  • Single canonical join key. The CBE-as-spine is exactly right for this domain. It keeps the data model intuitive and avoids identity-resolution hell on the company side.
  • One database, no exotic stack. Postgres + a few extensions does search, financials, vectors, and queues. No Elasticsearch, Kafka, Redis-required pieces. At this scale, that's the right call — operational simplicity beats theoretical purity.
  • Pre-pivoted reads. financial_latest, financial_by_year, sector_percentiles, company_info — pivoting at write time is the right tradeoff for a read-heavy product.
  • Two-pipeline AI design. Cheap-and-bulk for the long tail, expensive-and-narrative on demand. Cost-controlled by a daily budget guard, not just hope.
  • SKIP LOCKED queues. The enrichment + Staatsblad backfill queues both use FOR UPDATE SKIP LOCKED. That's the textbook Postgres-as-queue pattern; it scales further than people assume.
  • Auth model is restrained. "Free users can read everything; limits hit only on costly actions" is a clean product principle that simplifies a lot of code. It's also explicitly defended in the docs, so future contributors won't drift.

What I'd flag as risk

High

Staging shares the production database

A destructive migration tested on staging runs against prod data. Any worker started in staging mode writes to the prod queue. The codebase mitigates this with router-level admin gates and a STAGING_MODE flag, but the mitigation is a guardrail on a loaded gun. A read-replica for staging — even a snapshot refreshed weekly — would close most of the risk for a small ongoing cost.

High

Single Postgres instance, no replica, no PITR proven

Everything funnels through one Postgres on a Hetzner VPS. If the disk dies, the product dies until restore. KBO and NBB can be reloaded; activity logs, user state, and any AI enrichment paid for cannot. A standby replica + periodic logical backups, even to S3-compatible object storage, would be cheap insurance.

Medium

NBB key rotation is a recurring tax

The CBSO subscription keys rotate without warning — two rotations landed in a single 24-hour window in April. There's a watchdog cron + auto-regen Playwright flow, but the upstream is fundamentally fragile. The rejected SDMX-migration spike confirmed there's no equivalent free alternative for per-company filings, so this stays a structural cost. Worth keeping the auto-rotation tested.

Medium

TierLimitMiddleware queries the DB on every request

Every /api/* hit reads activity_log to count today's requests. At current volume that's fine; at 10× the activity_log table would be the first hot spot. A small per-process LRU with a 30-second TTL, or a Redis counter, would absorb the burst cheaply.

Medium

Data quality is uneven, by design

NBB only has structured admin data since April 2022, only for entities required to file. Small commanditaire vennootschappen (form 612) have zero admin rows. The KBO licence prohibits using personal data for direct marketing — so any "list every director in Belgium" feature is a regulatory landmine. The product makes this trade well, but it shapes what features can ever ship.

Medium

People identity is ambiguous

With no national ID, two "Jan De Clercq"s in the same city are probably one row in DataSnoop and probably two people in real life. Staatsblad domicile data narrows this when present, but the re-extraction cron is still backfilling. The right long-term answer is a proper person-resolution layer with confidence scores; today's grouping by name+city is good enough but quietly merges some distinct people.

Low

Sync requests inside async routes

Some NBB-load paths block the FastAPI event loop while waiting on the NBB API. Tolerable today (the 3-wide concurrency cap and 1.5-second politeness mean the loop isn't busy anyway), but as more of the backend goes async-native, this becomes a measurable tail-latency source. Already on the tech-debt list.

Low

pgvector at the chosen dimensions

256-dim embeddings via HNSW (m=16, ef_construction=64) is fine today. Cosine recall on ~570K vectors is good enough for the search experience and HNSW build cost has been manageable. If the corpus grows past a few million embedded entities (e.g. embedding people too, or all events) HNSW build time and memory would become the constraint and the parameters would want re-tuning. Not a today problem. (Aside: staatsblad_event_embedding uses IVFFlat with lists=100 instead — different table, same pgvector extension.)

Low

A few "wide scan" admin endpoints

/stats/sectors pulls ~300k rows into Python before aggregating. Cheap to fix with a single GROUP BY at the SQL layer, and it would also cut admin-page load times. Not user-visible today but a paper cut.

The "if I could change one thing" list

  1. Carve out a read-replica for staging. Even a 24-hour-stale snapshot would let staging be genuinely safe to break things on. Today the operational discipline carries the load, and one tired Friday will eventually bite.
  2. Move activity_log writes off the request path. A 1ms write on every API call is a paper cut at low scale and a sharp knife at high scale. Buffered async writes, partitioned table, done.
  3. Promote person identity to a first-class entity. Right now a "person" is a string-with-attributes. A person table keyed by some derived ID (with confidence scores tying admin / shareholder / event rows to it) would unlock much better people-search and is genuinely needed for PE workflows.
  4. Schema-as-truth for AI columns. company_enrichment currently gets created at runtime via DDL ALTERs in a Python startup hook. That's fragile and surprising — a normal migration file would make the table's existence boring and predictable.

None of these are showstoppers. They're the kind of items a senior architect flags so they don't quietly become showstoppers six months from now.
